INTRODUCTION

What is Arita Ware?

Porcelain produced in Arita Town,
Saga Prefecture — Japan's oldest
porcelain.

In 1616, Korean potter Yi Sam-pyeong
discovered high-quality porcelain stone
at Izumiyama, marking the birth of
Japanese porcelain production.

1616

The Birth of Japanese Porcelain

Korean potter Yi Sam-pyeong (Japanese name: Kanagae Sanbei),
who came to Japan during the Korean campaigns, discovered
porcelain stone at Izumiyama in Arita and succeeded in firing Japan's first porcelain.

STYLE 01

Ko-Imari

The collective term for porcelain
exported to Europe from Imari Port
during the Edo period.

The lavish Kinrande (gold brocade)
style captivated European royalty
and greatly influenced Western
porcelain makers like Meissen.

STYLE 02

Kakiemon

Distinguished by its "Nigoshide"
(milky white) base.

Elegant compositions utilizing
empty space, with vivid overglaze
enamels in red, green, yellow, and blue.

The asymmetrical Japanese aesthetic
is highly acclaimed worldwide.

STYLE 03

Nabeshima

The finest porcelain fired at the
official kiln of the Nabeshima
clan of Saga Domain.

Created as gifts for the Shogun
and feudal lords with absolutely
no compromise in quality.

"Iro-Nabeshima" with its blue, red,
and green palette is exceptionally beautiful.

KILNS

Notable Kilns

Koransha

Founded 1879

A prestigious kiln established in the Meiji era. Renowned as an Imperial Household supplier with refined designs and exceptional quality.

Fukagawa Seiji

Founded 1894

Imperial Household Agency supplier. Gold medal winner at the Paris World Exposition. Famous for its distinctive "Fukagawa Blue."

Imaemon

Founded c. 1640

Inheritors of the Iro-Nabeshima tradition. The 14th generation was designated a Living National Treasure. Known for unique "Sumi-hajiki" technique.

Kakiemon Kiln

Founded c. 1640

Preservers of the Nigoshide technique. The 15th generation is a Living National Treasure. Internationally acclaimed for excellence.
